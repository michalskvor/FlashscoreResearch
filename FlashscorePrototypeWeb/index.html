Player Card 2.0

Research Background

The new player card aims to fix key shortcomings of the current design. This document provides context for the three moderated research sessions.

Goals of the New Player Card

  • ·
    The card doesn't provide a simple answer to how the player performed.
  • ·
    The card should be more adapted to the player's position.
  • ·
    Transparency of the rating and interpretation of statistics.

Basic Research Questions

  • Q
    When do users use the player card — post-match or live?
  • Q
    When do they check statistics on the player card and when do they go to the player stats table?
  • Q
    Is the new division of metrics into categories clear? Do users understand the category names?
  • Q
    Would users be interested in a simple evaluation of different aspects of a player's performance based on metrics?
  • Q
    Is it important for users to know which role the player was evaluated in and from which the rating was calculated?

Three Focus Areas

1. Comprehension of Advanced Bonuses — Do users understand the more advanced bonus rows such as Finishing and Shot stopping? Do they perceive the explanations as helpful even if they don't grasp the analytics?

2. Top Stats & Interactive Shot Map — Does contextualising key stats (with visual cues) help users understand performance better? Does the shot map provide added value?

3. Integration of Goals & Penalties into Bonuses — Is it clear to users how goals and scored penalties are reflected in the bonuses? How would users expect to see these events represented?

Player Card 2.0 · Study 1 of 3

Comprehension of Advanced Bonuses

Users open the match lineups and tap any player card. The focus is on the two more advanced bonus rows — Finishing and Shot stopping — which combine a human-readable label with an analytical second line. Do users understand them without any external explanation?

Open in Figma

Overview

Hypothesis

Users do not fully understand the two more advanced bonuses — Finishing and Shot stopping — even though the first line provides a simple, human-readable explanation and the second line is more analytical (e.g. "xGOT 0.92 − xG 0.2").

Entry point: finished match lineup screen. Users tap any player on the pitch to reveal their player card, then scroll to the bonus section.

Focus Areas

  • ·
    Do users find value in explanations of why the player was rated this way for these actions?
  • ·
    Do users perceive the explanation as helping them understand the evaluation, even if they don't get the analytics?

Research Tasks

  • 1
    Open the lineup and tap on Bernardo Silva (the player with the most bonus rows). Describe what you see in the bonuses section.
  • 2
    Look at the row "Finishing (non-penalty)" with the sub-label "xGOT 0.92 − xG 0.2". What do you think this tells you about the player?
  • 3
    Does the two-line format (short label + analytical detail) help you understand the bonus better, or is it confusing?
  • 4
    What do the green (+), red (−) and grey (bar chart) icons in front of each row communicate to you?
  • 5
    Now open the goalkeeper card (Trafford or Lecomte). How is their "Top events" different from outfield players? Is it clear what it shows?
  • 6
    Looking at the overall card — do the bonuses help you understand why the player received this rating?

Player Card 2.0 · Study 2 of 3

Top Stats & Interactive Shot Map

A variant of the player card with two additions: a Top Stats bar surfacing key numbers (Shots, xG, xGOT, On target) right below the rating card, and a Shot Map showing where shots were taken on the pitch. This study explores whether contextualising stats improves understanding for both casual and analytical users.

Open in Figma

Overview

Hypothesis

Top Stats: Presenting context for key numbers helps users understand player stats better. The card is divided into two parts for different audiences — Bonuses/Headlines for casual users, Categories for advanced/analytical users. The "Top Stats" section should serve both.

Shot Map: An interactive shot map will provide added value; users will find it useful to inspect individual shots and gain spatial context that raw numbers cannot convey.

Focus Areas

  • ·
    Does presenting context for numbers help users understand the stats better?
  • ·
    Do casual users find the Top Stats section helpful for quick understanding?
  • ·
    Do advanced users use the Categories for deeper analysis?
  • ·
    Do users find the interactive shot map helpful for understanding player performance?
  • ·
    Which aspects of the shot map are most / least useful?

Research Tasks

  • 1
    Below the rating card you can see a row of four numbers. What do you think each of them represents? Are these the stats you'd want to see first?
  • 2
    Look at the shot map below. What does it show? Can you tell which shots were on target and which weren't?
  • 3
    Does seeing where the shots were taken on the pitch change how you perceive the player's performance compared to just seeing the numbers?
  • 4
    Imagine you follow football casually. Does this card give you a quick enough summary, or is there still too much information?
  • 5
    Now imagine you are an analytical fan. Would you use the detailed stat tables below? What is missing?
  • 6
    What would you add, remove or change on this card?

Player Card 2.0 · Study 3 of 3

Integration of Goals & Penalties into Bonuses

An A/B/C comparison testing three approaches to surfacing goals and penalties. Variant A (current): goals appear as icons in Top events and also as a single "Goals" bonus row. Variant B: goals appear only in Top events — the bonus section shows other performance metrics only. Variant C: goals appear in Top events and the bonus section explicitly splits them into "Goals" and a named "Penalty goal" row. Switch between the tabs to compare.

Open in Figma

Overview

Hypothesis

It is unclear to users how goals and scored penalties are reflected in the bonuses section. Including events like goals in Bonuses (which are already present in Top Events) may be confusing — but leaving them out may cause users to not understand how the final rating was determined.

Both variants use Foden Phil's card from the Man City vs Fulham match (1 goal scored, yellow card).

Focus Areas

  • ·
    Does the current implementation (Variant A) effectively communicate the impact of goals and scored penalties in bonuses?
  • ·
    How would users expect to see goals and scored penalties represented?
  • ·
    Do users expect critical events (goals, assists, red cards) to be repeated under Bonuses?
  • ·
    Would omitting these events from Bonuses reduce transparency or increase confusion?

Research Tasks

  • 1
    Look at Variant A. The player scored a goal — where on this card can you find that information?
  • 2
    In Variant A, the bonus section doesn't mention the goal. Does that feel like something is missing, or is it fine?
  • 3
    Now switch to Variant B. The goal now appears in the bonus list as "Goal (open play)". Does this feel more complete or more cluttered?
  • 4
    Variant B distinguishes between "open play goal" and "penalty scored". Is that level of detail useful or unnecessary?
  • 5
    Do the bonuses help you understand why the player got this rating in both variants? Which one is clearer?
  • 6
    Which variant would you prefer to see in the app and why?
